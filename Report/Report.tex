	\section{Introduction}
		The F-22 Raptor uses all-moving horizontal tailplanes, also known as stabilators, to manage longitudinal stability and control. 
		
	    This report presents a mathematical model of the F-22 Raptor horizontal tail stabilator using MATLAB and Simulink. The modeling process begins with defining the system geometry.

	\subsection{Actuator location and function}
	
	The Horizontal Stabilator Actuator is located within the aft fuselage structure near the root of both stabilators. The actuator is housed internally to protect from heat generated by the engines and to maintain stealth due to heat generation and detection from exposed hinges. 
	
	Its function is to provide angular deflection of the Stabilator by linearly deflecting with hydraulic actuation. Based on open source data, deflection range is $-25^{\circ}$ to $30^{\circ}$ \autocite{DeflectionLimits}.

	\section{Extension vs deflection function}
	
	Based on the geometric layout of the actuator mechanism, a mathematical relationship between the linear extension of the actuator ($\Delta x$) and the resulting angular deflection of the stabilator ($\Delta \alpha$) can be established.
	
	Let us define the geometric parameters (fig. \ref {fig:relationanalytical-solution}):
	\begin{itemize}
		\item $a$ is the distance between the pin connecting the actuator to the fuselage and the center of the stabilator pivot shaft.
		\item $d$ is the distance between the fuselage pin and the actuator shaft pin at $0^\circ$ deflection.
		\item $\Delta x$ represents the actuator extension.
		\item $r$ is the radius from the pivot shaft center to the actuator pin (lever arm).
		\item $\alpha$ is the initial angle between the segment $a$ and the lever arm $r$ at the pivot shaft.
	\end{itemize}
	
	At the neutral position, the arrangement forms a right-angled triangle at the actuator pin connection. Using the Pythagorean theorem, the radius $r$ can be determined as:
	\begin{equation}
		r = \sqrt{a^2 - d^2}
	\end{equation}
	
	The initial angle $\alpha$ can be found using basic right-triangle trigonometry:
	\begin{equation}
		\alpha = \arcsin\left(\frac{d}{a}\right)
	\end{equation}
	
	When the actuator extends by $\Delta x$, the new length of the actuator assembly becomes $d + \Delta x$. The pin rotates along a circular trajectory (the pivot shaft), changing the internal angle at the pivot to $\alpha + \Delta \alpha$. 
	
	Applying the Law of Cosines to the newly formed oblique triangle with sides $a$, $r$, and $(d + \Delta x)$:
	\begin{equation}
		(d + \Delta x)^2 = a^2 + r^2 - 2ar \cos(\alpha - \Delta \alpha)
	\end{equation}
	
	To find the stabilator deflection angle $\Delta \alpha$ as a function of the actuator extension $\Delta x$, the Law of Cosines equation is rearranged to isolate $\cos(\alpha + \Delta \alpha)$:
	\begin{equation}
		-2ar \cos(\alpha - \Delta \alpha) = (d + \Delta x)^2 - a^2 - r^2
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		\cos(\alpha - \Delta \alpha) = \frac{a^2 + r^2 - (d + \Delta x)^2}{2ar}
	\end{equation}
	
	Taking the inverse cosine ($\arccos$) of both sides yields the total angle:
	\begin{equation}
		\alpha - \Delta \alpha = \arccos\left( \frac{a^2 + r^2 - (d + \Delta x)^2}{2ar} \right)
	\end{equation}
	
	Finally, subtracting the initial angle $\alpha$ gives the analytical solution for the stabilator's angular deflection:
	\begin{equation}
		\Delta \alpha = - \arccos\left( \frac{a^2 + r^2 - (d + \Delta x)^2}{2ar} \right) + \alpha
		\label{eq:analyt-relation}
	\end{equation}

	\section{Load modeling}
	
	After the geometry is established, it is necessary to calculate actuator loads to later be able to send proper control signals.
	
	\subsection{Reynolds' number calculation}
	
	To establish the F-22 stabilator's flight envelope for XFOIL simulations, operational Reynolds numbers at 60,000 ft were calculated using $Re = \frac{\rho V \bar{c}}{\mu}$. The Mean Aerodynamic Chord ($\bar{c}$) was first determined via MATLAB multi-panel geometric analysis. Using standard 60,000 ft atmospheric density ($\rho$) = 0.116 $\mathrm{kg/m^3}$ and dynamic viscosity ($\mu$) $1.422 \times 10^{-5} \ \mathrm{kg/(m \cdot s)}$, the Reynolds number was evaluated at two velocity extremes: a maximum speed ($V_{max}$) = 670.55 m/s derived from the aircraft's Mach 2.25 limit, and a stall speed ($V_{stall}$) = 213.987 m/s calculated by converting an estimated sea-level Equivalent Airspeed to True Airspeed via the density ratio method. The values obtained were ($Re_{max}$) = 1.6457 e+07 and ($Re_{min}$) = 5.2517 e+06.
	
	\subsection{Airfoil and aerodynamic coefficients}
	

    The first step in load modelling is to determine the aerodynamic coefficients of the airfoil, as these directly influence the forces acting on the structure. In this study, the aerodynamic coefficients were obtained by analyzing the NACA 0010 airfoil, which is the airfoil that is reasonably similar to F 22's stabilator according to literature,\autocite{Stabilatorairfoil} using XFOIL. XFOIL is a panel method-based software that combines inviscid flow calculations with boundary layer analysis to predict lift, drag, and moment coefficients with good accuracy.
    For this analysis, the NACA 0010 airfoil was evaluated over a wide range of angles of attack, from $-25^\circ$ to $30^\circ$. Simulations were conducted for Reynolds numbers at maximum and minimum speed at 60k ft. The resulting data provides a set of lift, drag, and moment coefficients, which serve as essential inputs for subsequent load modelling.

	\subsection{External Loads as a Function of Deflection Angle and Velocity}
	
	The external aerodynamic loads depend on the dynamic pressure ($q$) and the aerodynamic coefficients ($C_L$, $C_D$, $C_m$). In the simulated model, these coefficients are not solely functions of the stabilator deflection angle ($\Delta \alpha$), but are also interpolated based on velocity ($V$) to account for Reynolds number variations across the flight envelope.
	
	First, the dynamic pressure is defined as:
	\begin{equation}
		q = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2
	\end{equation}
	
	The aerodynamic coefficients are determined by linearly interpolating between low and high Reynolds number datasets using a velocity fraction ($f_V$):
	\begin{equation}
		f_V = \frac{V - V_{min}}{V_{max} - V_{min}}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		C_{i}(\Delta \alpha, V) = C_{i, lRe}(\Delta \alpha) + f_V \cdot \left( C_{i, hRe}(\Delta \alpha) - C_{i, lRe}(\Delta \alpha) \right)
	\end{equation}
	where $i$ represents $L$, $D$, or $m$.
	
	Next, the equations for the aerodynamic forces and moment acting at the aerodynamic center (25\% of the Mean Aerodynamic Chord, or MAC) are formulated as:
	\begin{align}
		F_y(\Delta \alpha, V) &= C_L(\Delta \alpha, V) \cdot S_{stab} \cdot q \\
		F_x(\Delta \alpha, V) &= C_D(\Delta \alpha, V) \cdot S_{stab} \cdot q \\
		M_{stab}(\Delta \alpha, V) &= C_m(\Delta \alpha, V) \cdot S_{stab} \cdot \text{MAC} \cdot q
	\end{align}
	
	\subsection{Equilibrium Equations}
	
	For the stabilator to remain in static equilibrium at a given deflection angle, the moment generated by the actuator around the pivot shaft must perfectly balance the total aerodynamic moment acting on that same pivot shaft ($\sum M = 0$). Let $L_{arm}$ be the distance between the aerodynamic center (which is at 25\% of MAC from the leading edge for NACA0010 \autocite{report824}) and the pivot axis.
	
	The equilibrium equations are:
	\begin{gather}
		M_{pivot} = M_{stab} + F_y \cdot L_{arm} \cos(\Delta \alpha) + F_x \cdot L_{arm} \sin(\Delta \alpha) \\
		R_{pivot \ x} + F_x = 0 \\
		R_{pivot \ y} + F_y = 0
	\end{gather}
	
	\subsection{Equation Relating Actuator Load to External Loads}
	
	To find the actuator force ($F_{act}$), its mechanical advantage, or the perpendicular lever arm ($h_{act}$) from the pivot shaft to the actuator's line of action must be determined. Let $L_{act} = d + \Delta x$ (fig. \ref{fig:relationanalytical-solution}). Using Heron's formula, the area of the triangle formed by $L_{act}$, $a$, and $r$ can be found to determine the height ($h_{act}$):
	
	Calculate the semi-perimeter ($s$):
	\begin{equation}
		s = \frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2}
	\end{equation}
	
	Calculate the area of the triangle:
	\begin{equation}
		\text{Area} = \sqrt{s(s - L_{act})(s - a)(s - r)}
	\end{equation}
	
	Calculate the perpendicular lever arm ($h_{act}$):
	\begin{equation}
		h_{act} = \frac{2 \cdot \text{Area}}{L_{act}}
	\end{equation}
	
	Finally, relate the actuator load to the external load (the pivot moment) using the calculated lever arm:
	\begin{equation}
		F_{act} = \frac{M_{pivot}}{h_{act}}
	\end{equation}
	
	Without the intermediate $M_{pivot}$ and $h_{act}$ values the equation takes such a form:
	\begin{equation}
		F_{act} = \frac{M_{stab} + F_y \cdot L_{arm} \cos(\Delta \alpha) + F_x \cdot L_{arm} \sin(\Delta \alpha)}{\frac{2 \cdot \sqrt{\frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2}(\frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2} - L_{act})(\frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2} - a)(\frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2} - r)}}{L_{act}}}
	\end{equation}

	\section{Actuator modeling}
	
	The control signal must be converted into actuator deflection. For this, it is necessary to define the model of the actuator. To assure plausibility of the simulation, a real commercially available actuator that satisfies the military-grade requirements will be chosen.
	
	\subsection{Actuator model}
	
	The actuator architecture selected for this system is an Electro-Hydrostatic Actuator (EHA), specifically the Fixed Pump displacement and Variable Motor speed (FPVM) EHA configuration \autocite{ActuatormodelSimulink}. In this setup, a bi-directional hydraulic pump is driven by a Brushless DC Motor (BLDCM), which rotates at variable speeds and directions to control the oil flow and supply pressure to a symmetrical hydraulic actuator. To maintain closed-circuit operation and compensate for fluid leakage, a refeeding circuit consisting of an accumulator and check valves is integrated into the system.
	
	The mathematical model of the FPVM-EHA can be decomposed into three primary subsystems: the BLDCM, the hydraulic pump, and the hydraulic jack.
	
	\subsubsection{Motor Dynamics}
	The Brushless DC Motor acts as the primary driver for the pump. The electrical and mechanical governing equations for the motor are expressed as follows:
	\begin{equation}
		U_{c} = E + L\frac{di}{dt} + Ri
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		E = K_{e}\omega
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		T_{e} = K_{t}i
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		T_{e} = J\dot{\omega} + T_{f} + T_{l}
	\end{equation}
	where $U_c$ is the command voltage, $i$ is the current, $E$ is the back electromotive force, $T_e$ is the electromagnetic torque, $\omega$ is the rotational speed, and $J$ represents the total inertia of the motor and pump.
	
	\subsubsection{Pump Flow Equations}
	The flow equations for the pump outlet ($Q_1$) and entry ($Q_2$) account for the pump displacement ($D$), internal leakage ($K_{ilp}$), and external leakage ($K_{elp}$) relative to the accumulator pressure ($P_{ac}$):
	\begin{equation}
		Q_{1} = D\omega - K_{ilp}(P_{1} - P_{2}) - K_{elp}(P_{1} - P_{ac})
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		Q_{2} = D\omega - K_{ilp}(P_{1} - P_{2}) + K_{elp}(P_{2} - P_{ac})
	\end{equation}
	
	\subsubsection{Refeeding Circuit and Accumulator}
	To maintain the closed-circuit integrity of the EHA, a refeeding circuit comprising an accumulator and check valves is utilized. The accumulator pressure ($P_{ac}$) is modeled using a polytropic gas equation to account for fluid compression and expansion:
	\begin{equation}
		P_{ac} = \frac{P_{aci} V_{gasi}^k}{(V_{gasi} - \int Q_{ac} dt)^k}
	\end{equation}
	where $P_{aci}$ is the initial accumulator pressure, $V_{gasi}$ is the initial gas volume, $k$ is the polytropic exponent, and $Q_{ac}$ is the net flow rate into the accumulator. The check valves manage the flow between the accumulator and the main hydraulic lines based on the pressure differentials $(P_1 - P_{ac})$ and $(P_2 - P_{ac})$.
	
	\subsubsection{Chamber Flow Equations}
	To connect the fluid flow from the pump to the pressure acting on the piston, the flow continuity equations for the two working chambers of the hydraulic jack must be established. These equations account for the actuator's linear velocity, fluid compressibility, and internal leakage across the piston:
	\begin{equation}
		Q_{1f} = A\dot{x}_{t} + \frac{V_{10} + Ax_{t}}{B}\dot{P}_{1} + K_{ilj}(P_{1} - P_{2})
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		Q_{2f} = A\dot{x}_{t} - \frac{V_{20} - Ax_{t}}{B}\dot{P}_{2} + K_{ilj}(P_{1} - P_{2})
	\end{equation}
	where $Q_{1f}$ and $Q_{2f}$ are the flows into the respective chambers, $A$ is the active piston area, $x_{t}$ and $\dot{x}_{t}$ are the piston displacement and velocity, $V_{10}$ and $V_{20}$ are the initial chamber volumes, $B$ is the fluid bulk modulus, and $K_{ilj}$ is the internal leakage coefficient of the hydraulic jack.
	
	\subsubsection{Hydraulic Jack and Load Balance}
	The EHA utilizes a symmetrical hydraulic jack to ensure proper flow balance between the actuator and the pump. The load force balance equation acting on the piston is defined by the pressure difference across the active area ($A$), the equivalent mass of the piston and load ($M$), the external aerodynamic load force ($F_{ex}$), and the system friction ($F_{fric}$):
	\begin{equation}
		A(P_{1} - P_{2}) = M\ddot{x}_{t} + F_{ex} + F_{fric}
	\end{equation}
	where $\ddot{x}_{t}$ represents the linear acceleration of the actuator.
	
	\subsubsection{Friction Model}
	An accurate representation of friction is critical for predicting the dynamic performance of the actuator, particularly around zero velocity. A continuous Stribeck friction model is employed to avoid numerical discontinuities during simulation:
	\begin{equation}
		F_{fric}(\dot{x}_{t}) = \left[ F_{c} + (F_{s} - F_{c}) \cdot e^{-|\dot{x}_{t}|/\alpha} + K_{vis}|\dot{x}_{t}| \right] \cdot \tanh\left(\frac{\dot{x}_{t}}{\beta}\right)
	\end{equation}
	where $F_c$ is the Coulomb friction, $F_s$ is the maximum static friction, $K_{vis}$ is the viscous friction coefficient, $\dot{x}_{t}$ is the piston velocity, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are reference speed parameters used to smooth the transitions near zero velocity.
	
	\subsection{Required actuator parameters}
	
	To select a Commercial Off-The-Shelf (COTS) Electro-Hydrostatic Actuator (EHA) suitable for this simulation, the minimum kinematic requirements must first be established based on the F-22's geometry and low-speed maneuverability needs.
	
	\subsubsection{Kinematic Requirements}
	Regardless of high-speed aerodynamic limiting, the actuator must physically be capable of extending and retracting enough to cover the full stabilator deflection range ($-25^\circ$ to $30^\circ$) during low-speed flight and ground operations. The total required stroke ($\Delta x_{total}$) is:
	\begin{equation}
		\Delta x_{total} = \Delta x_{max} - \Delta x_{min} = 163.61 \text{ mm}
	\end{equation}
	
	A deflection rate limit has to be assumed to define the dynamic requirement. The value of $\dot{\alpha}_{max} = 60^\circ/\text{s}$ is assumed since it is a standard limit among American fighter jets \autocite{deflectionrate}, although the data for the F-22 specifically is not available.
	
	The linear slew rate ($v_{act}$) dictates how fast the actuator must extend or retract to achieve this angular rotation rate. The maximum linear velocity is calculated using the highest rate of change of the extension with respect to the deflection angle:
	\begin{equation}
		v_{act} = \max \left| \frac{d(\Delta x)}{d(\Delta \alpha)} \right| \cdot \dot{\alpha}_{max} = 185.84 \text{ mm/s}
	\end{equation}
	
	\subsubsection{Load and Envelope Limiting Strategy}
	The theoretical structural loads ($F_{act}$) and corresponding power requirements ($P_{mech, max}$ and $P_{elec, max}$) are mathematically derived by evaluating the absolute extremes across all simulated velocities and deflection angles. 
	
	These theoretical requirements for the EHA are presented in Table \ref{table:hydro_reqs}. However, calculating hinge moments at maximum velocity (Mach 2.25) and maximum deflection ($30^\circ$) yields theoretical loads exceeding 1.18 MN and electrical power demands approaching 288 kW. In reality, such deflections at maximum dynamic pressure are impractical and would cause catastrophic structural failure. Modern flight control systems employ \enquote{blowdown limits} to actively restrict control surface deflection at high speeds.
	
	For that reason, rather than sizing a theoretical actuator to meet impossible worst-case loads, an actuator produced by typical American military suppliers (e.g., Moog or Parker Aerospace) that meets the practical kinematic requirements will be selected. After that, the allowed deflection angles at different speeds will be calculated based on the maximum load capabilities of that specific actuator.
