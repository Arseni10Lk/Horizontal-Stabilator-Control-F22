	\section{Introduction}
		The F-22 Raptor uses all-moving horizontal tailplanes, also known as stabilators, to manage longitudinal stability and control. 
		
	    This report presents a mathematical model of the F-22 Raptor horizontal tail stabilator using MATLAB and Simulink. The modeling process begins with defining the system geometry.

	\subsection{Actuator location and function}
	
	The Horizontal Stabilator Actuator is located within the aft fuselage structure near the root of both stabilators. The actuator is housed internally to protect from heat generated by the engines and to maintain stealth due to heat generation and detection from exposed hinges. 
	
	Its function is to provide angular deflection of the Stabilator by linearly deflecting with hydraulic actuation. Based on open source data, deflection range is $-25^{\circ}$ to $30^{\circ}$ \autocite{DeflectionLimits}.

	\section{Extension vs deflection function}
		Based on the geometric layout of the actuator mechanism, a mathematical relationship between the linear extension of the actuator ($\Delta x$) and the resulting angular deflection of the stabilator ($\Delta \alpha$) can be established.
	
	Let us define the geometric parameters (fig. \ref {fig:relationanalytical-solution}):
	\begin{itemize}
		\item $a$ is the distance between the pin connecting the actuator to the fuselage and the center of the stabilator pivot shaft.
		\item $d$ is the distance between the fuselage pin and the actuator shaft pin at $0^\circ$ deflection.
		\item $\Delta x$ represents the actuator extension.
		\item $r$ is the radius from the pivot shaft center to the actuator pin (lever arm).
		\item $\alpha$ is the initial angle between the segment $a$ and the lever arm $r$ at the pivot shaft.
	\end{itemize}
	
	At the neutral position, the arrangement forms a right-angled triangle at the actuator pin connection. Using the Pythagorean theorem, the radius $r$ can be determined as:
	\begin{equation}
		r = \sqrt{a^2 - d^2}
	\end{equation}
	
	The initial angle $\alpha$ can be found using basic right-triangle trigonometry:
	\begin{equation}
		\alpha = \arcsin\left(\frac{d}{a}\right)
	\end{equation}
	
	When the actuator extends by $\Delta x$, the new length of the actuator assembly becomes $d + \Delta x$. The pin rotates along a circular trajectory (the pivot shaft), changing the internal angle at the pivot to $\alpha + \Delta \alpha$. 
	
	Applying the Law of Cosines to the newly formed oblique triangle with sides $a$, $r$, and $(d + \Delta x)$:
	\begin{equation}
		(d + \Delta x)^2 = a^2 + r^2 - 2ar \cos(\alpha - \Delta \alpha)
	\end{equation}
	
	To find the stabilator deflection angle $\Delta \alpha$ as a function of the actuator extension $\Delta x$, the Law of Cosines equation is rearranged to isolate $\cos(\alpha + \Delta \alpha)$:
	\begin{equation}
		-2ar \cos(\alpha - \Delta \alpha) = (d + \Delta x)^2 - a^2 - r^2
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		\cos(\alpha - \Delta \alpha) = \frac{a^2 + r^2 - (d + \Delta x)^2}{2ar}
	\end{equation}
	
	Taking the inverse cosine ($\arccos$) of both sides yields the total angle:
	\begin{equation}
		\alpha - \Delta \alpha = \arccos\left( \frac{a^2 + r^2 - (d + \Delta x)^2}{2ar} \right)
	\end{equation}
	
	Finally, subtracting the initial angle $\alpha$ gives the analytical solution for the stabilator's angular deflection:
	\begin{equation}
		\Delta \alpha = - \arccos\left( \frac{a^2 + r^2 - (d + \Delta x)^2}{2ar} \right) + \alpha
		\label{eq:analyt-relation}
	\end{equation}

	\section{Load modeling}
	
	After the geometry is established, it is necessary to calculate actuator loads to later be able to send proper control signals.
	
	\subsection{Reynolds' number calculation}
	
	To establish the F-22 stabilator's flight envelope for XFOIL simulations, operational Reynolds numbers at 60,000 ft were calculated using $Re = \frac{\rho V \bar{c}}{\mu}$. The Mean Aerodynamic Chord ($\bar{c}$) was first determined via MATLAB multi-panel geometric analysis. Using standard 60,000 ft atmospheric density ($\rho$) = 0.116 $\mathrm{kg/m^3}$ and dynamic viscosity ($\mu$) $1.422 \times 10^{-5} \ \mathrm{kg/(m \cdot s)}$, the Reynolds number was evaluated at two velocity extremes: a maximum speed ($V_{max}$) = 670.55 m/s derived from the aircraft's Mach 2.25 limit, and a stall speed ($V_{stall}$) = 213.987 m/s calculated by converting an estimated sea-level Equivalent Airspeed to True Airspeed via the density ratio method. The values obtained were ($Re_{max}$) = 1.6457 e+07 and ($Re_{min}$) = 5.2517 e+06.
	
	\subsection{Airfoil and aerodynamic coefficients}
	

    The first step in load modelling is to determine the aerodynamic coefficients of the airfoil, as these directly influence the forces acting on the structure. In this study, the aerodynamic coefficients were obtained by analyzing the NACA 0010 airfoil, which is the airfoil that is reasonably similar to F 22's stabilator according to literature,\autocite{Stabilatorairfoil} using XFOIL. XFOIL is a panel method-based software that combines inviscid flow calculations with boundary layer analysis to predict lift, drag, and moment coefficients with good accuracy.
    For this analysis, the NACA 0010 airfoil was evaluated over a wide range of angles of attack, from $-25^\circ$ to $30^\circ$. Simulations were conducted for Reynolds numbers at maximum and minimum speed at 60k ft. The resulting data provides a set of lift, drag, and moment coefficients, which serve as essential inputs for subsequent load modelling.

	\subsection{External Loads as a Function of Deflection Angle and Velocity}
	
	The external aerodynamic loads depend on the dynamic pressure ($q$) and the aerodynamic coefficients ($C_L$, $C_D$, $C_m$). In the simulated model, these coefficients are not solely functions of the stabilator deflection angle ($\Delta \alpha$), but are also interpolated based on velocity ($V$) to account for Reynolds number variations across the flight envelope.
	
	First, the dynamic pressure is defined as:
	\begin{equation}
		q = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2
	\end{equation}
	
	The aerodynamic coefficients are determined by linearly interpolating between low and high Reynolds number datasets using a velocity fraction ($f_V$):
	\begin{equation}
		f_V = \frac{V - V_{min}}{V_{max} - V_{min}}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		C_{i}(\Delta \alpha, V) = C_{i, lRe}(\Delta \alpha) + f_V \cdot \left( C_{i, hRe}(\Delta \alpha) - C_{i, lRe}(\Delta \alpha) \right)
	\end{equation}
	where $i$ represents $L$, $D$, or $m$.
	
	Next, the equations for the aerodynamic forces and moment acting at the aerodynamic center (25\% of the Mean Aerodynamic Chord, or MAC) are formulated as:
	\begin{align}
		F_y(\Delta \alpha, V) &= C_L(\Delta \alpha, V) \cdot S_{stab} \cdot q \\
		F_x(\Delta \alpha, V) &= C_D(\Delta \alpha, V) \cdot S_{stab} \cdot q \\
		M_{stab}(\Delta \alpha, V) &= C_m(\Delta \alpha, V) \cdot S_{stab} \cdot \text{MAC} \cdot q
	\end{align}
	
	\subsection{Equilibrium Equations}
	
	For the stabilator to remain in static equilibrium at a given deflection angle, the moment generated by the actuator around the pivot shaft must perfectly balance the total aerodynamic moment acting on that same pivot shaft ($\sum M = 0$). Let $L_{arm}$ be the distance between the aerodynamic center (which is at 25\% of MAC from the leading edge for NACA0010 \autocite{report824}) and the pivot axis.
	
	The equilibrium equations are:
	\begin{gather}
		M_{pivot} = M_{stab} + F_y \cdot L_{arm} \cos(\Delta \alpha) + F_x \cdot L_{arm} \sin(\Delta \alpha) \\
		R_{pivot \ x} + F_x = 0 \\
		R_{pivot \ y} + F_y = 0
	\end{gather}
	
	\subsection{Equation Relating Actuator Load to External Loads}
	
	To find the actuator force ($F_{act}$), its mechanical advantage, or the perpendicular lever arm ($h_{act}$) from the pivot shaft to the actuator's line of action must be determined. Let $L_{act} = d + \Delta x$ (fig. \ref{fig:relationanalytical-solution}). Using Heron's formula, the area of the triangle formed by $L_{act}$, $a$, and $r$ can be found to determine the height ($h_{act}$):
	
	Calculate the semi-perimeter ($s$):
	\begin{equation}
		s = \frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2}
	\end{equation}
	
	Calculate the area of the triangle:
	\begin{equation}
		\text{Area} = \sqrt{s(s - L_{act})(s - a)(s - r)}
	\end{equation}
	
	Calculate the perpendicular lever arm ($h_{act}$):
	\begin{equation}
		h_{act} = \frac{2 \cdot \text{Area}}{L_{act}}
	\end{equation}
	
	Finally, relate the actuator load to the external load (the pivot moment) using the calculated lever arm:
	\begin{equation}
		F_{act} = \frac{M_{pivot}}{h_{act}}
	\end{equation}
	
	Without the intermediate $M_{pivot}$ and $h_{act}$ values the equation takes such a form:
	\begin{equation}
		F_{act} = \frac{M_{stab} + F_y \cdot L_{arm} \cos(\Delta \alpha) + F_x \cdot L_{arm} \sin(\Delta \alpha)}{\frac{2 \cdot \sqrt{\frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2}(\frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2} - L_{act})(\frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2} - a)(\frac{L_{act} + a + r}{2} - r)}}{L_{act}}}
	\end{equation}

	\section{Actuator modeling}
	
	The control signal must be converted into actuator deflection. For this, it is necessary to define the model of the actuator. To assure plausibility of the simulation, a real commercially available actuator that satisfies the military-grade requirements will be chosen.
	
	\subsection{Actuator model}
	
	The actuator architecture selected for this system is an Electro-Hydrostatic Actuator (EHA), specifically the Fixed Pump displacement and Variable Motor speed (FPVM) EHA configuration \autocite{ActuatormodelSimulink}. In this setup, a bi-directional hydraulic pump is driven by a DC Motor, which rotates at variable speeds and directions to control the oil flow and supply pressure to a symmetrical hydraulic actuator. To maintain closed-circuit operation and compensate for fluid leakage, a refeeding circuit consisting of an accumulator and check valves is integrated into the system. (see fig. \ref{fig:simulinkactuatormodel})
	
	The mathematical model of the FPVM-EHA can be decomposed into three primary subsystems: the DC motor, the hydraulic pump, and the hydraulic jack.

		\label{fig:simulinkactuatormodel}
	\end{figure}
	
	\subsubsection{Motor Dynamics}
	The DC Motor acts as the primary driver for the pump. The electrical and mechanical governing equations for the motor are expressed as follows:
	\begin{equation}
		U_{c} = E + L\frac{di}{dt} + Ri
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		E = K_{e}\omega
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		T_{e} = K_{t}i
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		T_{e} = J\dot{\omega} + T_{f} + T_{l}
	\end{equation}
	where $U_c$ is the command voltage, $i$ is the current, $E$ is the back electromotive force, $T_e$ is the electromagnetic torque, $\omega$ is the rotational speed, and $J$ represents the total inertia of the motor and pump.
	
	\subsubsection{Pump Flow Equations}
	The flow equations for the pump outlet ($Q_1$) and entry ($Q_2$) account for the pump displacement ($D$), internal leakage ($K_{ilp}$), and external leakage ($K_{elp}$) relative to the accumulator pressure ($P_{ac}$):
	\begin{equation}
		Q_{1} = D\omega - K_{ilp}(P_{1} - P_{2}) - K_{elp}(P_{1} - P_{ac})
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		Q_{2} = D\omega - K_{ilp}(P_{1} - P_{2}) + K_{elp}(P_{2} - P_{ac})
	\end{equation}
	
	\subsubsection{Refeeding Circuit and Accumulator}
	To maintain the closed-circuit integrity of the EHA, a refeeding circuit comprising an accumulator and check valves is utilized. The accumulator pressure ($P_{ac}$) is modeled using a polytropic gas equation to account for fluid compression and expansion:
	\begin{equation}
		P_{ac} = \frac{P_{aci} V_{gasi}^k}{(V_{gasi} - \int Q_{ac} dt)^k}
	\end{equation}
	where $P_{aci}$ is the initial accumulator pressure, $V_{gasi}$ is the initial gas volume, $k$ is the polytropic exponent, and $Q_{ac}$ is the net flow rate into the accumulator. The check valves manage the flow between the accumulator and the main hydraulic lines based on the pressure differentials $(P_1 - P_{ac})$ and $(P_2 - P_{ac})$.
	
	\subsubsection{Chamber Flow Equations}
	To connect the fluid flow from the pump to the pressure acting on the piston, the flow continuity equations for the two working chambers of the hydraulic jack must be established. These equations account for the actuator's linear velocity, fluid compressibility, and internal leakage across the piston:
	\begin{equation}
		Q_{1f} = A\dot{x}_{t} + \frac{V_{10} + Ax_{t}}{B}\dot{P}_{1} + K_{ilj}(P_{1} - P_{2})
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		Q_{2f} = A\dot{x}_{t} - \frac{V_{20} - Ax_{t}}{B}\dot{P}_{2} + K_{ilj}(P_{1} - P_{2})
	\end{equation}
	where $Q_{1f}$ and $Q_{2f}$ are the flows into the respective chambers, $A$ is the active piston area, $x_{t}$ and $\dot{x}_{t}$ are the piston displacement and velocity, $V_{10}$ and $V_{20}$ are the initial chamber volumes, $B$ is the fluid bulk modulus, and $K_{ilj}$ is the internal leakage coefficient of the hydraulic jack.
	
	\subsubsection{Hydraulic Jack and Load Balance}
	The EHA utilizes a symmetrical hydraulic jack to ensure proper flow balance between the actuator and the pump. The load force balance equation acting on the piston is defined by the pressure difference across the active area ($A$), the equivalent mass of the piston and load ($M$), the external aerodynamic load force ($F_{ex}$), and the system friction ($F_{fric}$):
	\begin{equation}
		A(P_{1} - P_{2}) = M\ddot{x}_{t} + F_{ex} + F_{fric}
		\label{eq:jack_eq}
	\end{equation}
	where $\ddot{x}_{t}$ represents the linear acceleration of the actuator.
	
	\subsubsection{Friction Model}
	An accurate representation of friction is critical for predicting the dynamic performance of the actuator, particularly around zero velocity. A continuous Stribeck friction model is employed to avoid numerical discontinuities during simulation:
	\begin{equation}
		F_{fric}(\dot{x}_{t}) = \left[ F_{c} + (F_{s} - F_{c}) \cdot e^{-|\dot{x}_{t}|/\alpha} + K_{vis}|\dot{x}_{t}| \right] \cdot \tanh\left(\frac{\dot{x}_{t}}{\beta}\right)
	\end{equation}
	where $F_c$ is the Coulomb friction, $F_s$ is the maximum static friction, $K_{vis}$ is the viscous friction coefficient, $\dot{x}_{t}$ is the piston velocity, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are reference speed parameters used to smooth the transitions near zero velocity.
	
	\subsection{Required actuator parameters}
	
	To select a Commercial Off-The-Shelf (COTS) Electro-Hydrostatic Actuator (EHA) suitable for this simulation, the minimum kinematic requirements must first be established based on the F-22's geometry and low-speed maneuverability needs.
	
	\subsubsection{Kinematic Requirements}
	Regardless of high-speed aerodynamic limiting, the actuator must physically be capable of extending and retracting enough to cover the full stabilator deflection range ($-25^\circ$ to $30^\circ$) during low-speed flight and ground operations. The total required stroke ($\Delta x_{total}$) is:
	\begin{equation}
		\Delta x_{total} = \Delta x_{max} - \Delta x_{min} = 75.1 \text{ mm} - (-88.5) \text{ mm} = 163.61 \text{ mm}
	\end{equation}
	
	A deflection rate limit has to be assumed to define the dynamic requirement. The value of $\dot{\alpha}_{max} = 60^\circ/\text{s}$ is assumed since it is a standard limit among American fighter jets \autocite{deflectionrate}, although the data for the F-22 specifically is not available.
	
	The linear slew rate ($v_{act}$) dictates how fast the actuator must extend or retract to achieve this angular rotation rate. The maximum linear velocity is calculated using the highest rate of change of the extension with respect to the deflection angle:
	
	\begin{equation}
		v_{act} = \max \left| \frac{d(\Delta x)}{d(\Delta \alpha)} \right| \cdot \dot{\alpha}_{max} = 185.84 \text{ mm/s}
	\end{equation}
	\vspace{0.5cm}
	
	\subsubsection{Load and Envelope Limiting Strategy}
	The theoretical structural loads ($F_{act}$) and corresponding power requirements ($P_{mech, max}$ and $P_{elec, max}$) are mathematically derived by evaluating the absolute extremes across all simulated velocities and deflection angles. 
	
	These theoretical requirements for the EHA are presented in Table \ref{table:hydro_reqs}. However, calculating hinge moments at maximum velocity (Mach 2.25) and maximum deflection ($30^\circ$) yields theoretical loads exceeding 1.18 MN and electrical power demands approaching 288 kW. In reality, such deflections at maximum dynamic pressure are impractical and would cause catastrophic structural failure. Modern flight control systems employ \enquote{blowdown limits} to actively restrict control surface deflection at high speeds.
	
	For that reason, rather than sizing a theoretical actuator to meet impossible worst-case loads, an actuator produced by typical American military suppliers (e.g., Moog or Parker Aerospace) that meets the practical kinematic requirements will be selected. After that, the allowed deflection angles at different speeds will be calculated based on the maximum load capabilities of that specific actuator.

    	\subsubsection{Calculations}
		
		The following calculations assume ideal hydraulic efficiency and constant pressure operation.
     
        The actuator force is determined from the piston and annular areas. For a bore diameter of $D = 0.2032\,\text{m}$ and rod diameter $d = 0.0889\,\text{m}$:
     
    	\begin{equation}
		A_{comp} = \frac{\pi \cdot D^2}{4}\approx 0.0324\,\text{m}^2
    	\end{equation}
    	
	    The tensile (annular) area is given by:
	    \begin{equation}
		A_{tensile} = A_{comp}- A_{rod} = 0.0324\,\text{m}^2 - 0.0062\,\text{m}^2 \approx 0.0262\,\text{m}^2
    	\end{equation}
    	
    	At an operating pressure of $20,680\,\text{kPa}$, the resulting forces are:
    	\begin{equation}
		F_{comp} = P \cdot A_{comp} = 20,680\,\text{kPa} \cdot 0.0324\,\text{m}^2 \approx 670.297\,\text{kN}
	   \end{equation}
		\begin{equation}
		F_{tensile} = P \cdot A_{tensile} = 20,680\,\text{kPa} \cdot 0.0262\,\text{m}^2 \approx 541.998\,\text{kN}
	    \end{equation}
	    
    	\textbf{Kinematics and Power}
	    The mechanical power ($P_{mech}$) is the product of the force and the velocity (slew rate). 
	    \begin{equation}
		P_{mech} = F_{comp} \cdot v = 670.297\,\text{kN} \cdot 0.1858\,\text{m/s} \approx 124.568\,\text{kW}
    	\end{equation}
    	The required hydraulic flow ($Q$) in metric units to maintain the slew rate:
	 \begin{equation}
		Q = A_{comp} \cdot v = 0.0324\,\text{m}^2 \cdot 0.1858\,\text{m/s} \approx 6.0236\,\text{L/s}
	 \end{equation}

		\textbf{Function in the EHA Circuit}
		
		The accumulator serves three functions within the EHA hydraulic circuit:
		
		\begin{enumerate}
			\item \textbf{Peak flow assist:} During rapid maneuver commands, instantaneous flow demand may transiently exceed pump delivery. The accumulator releases stored energy to bridge this gap without requiring the motor to overspeed.
			\item \textbf{Pressure ripple damping:} The accumulator absorbs pulses and presents a stable pressure to the Model 450 servo valve, preventing dynamic excitation of the valve spool.
			\item \textbf{Failsafe pressure maintenance:} In the event of a momentary motor power interruption, the accumulator sustains system pressure for a short duration, allowing the flight control system to execute a safe handover or hold command.
		\end{enumerate}
		
		\textbf{Type Selection}
		
		Parker offers three hydropneumatic accumulator types: bladder, diaphragm, and piston. For this application a piston accumulator is selected. Bladder accumulators offer fast response but have limited flow rates, diaphragm accumulators are too small in volume for the energy storage required. The piston type accommodates high flow rates, wide operating temperature ranges, and large pressure ratios  all required characteristics for a military flight control EHA.\autocite{parker_accumulator_choice}\autocite{parker_ehp_europe}
		
		\textbf{Selected Series: Parker EHP}
		
		The Parker EHP piston accumulator series is manufactured in carbon and stainless steel and is rated to a maximum working pressure of 350 bar. At the system operating pressure of 207 bar, the EHP series provides a pressure safety factor of approximately 1.7.

	\subsection{Stabilator Description}
	The structural mass of the F-22 horizontal stabilator was estimated by modeling the geometry as a multi-panel tapered wing section. Given the high-speed requirements and thermal environment of the aircraft, a sandwich construction was assumed, consisting of a titanium honeycomb core and carbon fiber bismaleimide (BMI) skins.
	
	The internal volume $V$ of the stabilator is calculated by integrating the cross-sectional area of the NACA 0010 airfoil across the span. For a tapered panel, the volume is given by:
	\begin{equation}
		V_{panel} = \kappa \cdot t_{ratio} \cdot b \cdot \frac{C_r^2 + C_r C_t + C_t^2}{3}
	\end{equation}
	where $\kappa$ is the airfoil cross-sectional factor ($0.685$ for NACA 00XX series), $t_{ratio}$ is the thickness-to-chord ratio ($0.10$), and $b$, $C_r$, and $C_t$ are the span, root chord, and tip chord respectively.
	
	To account for the non-uniform density distribution, the total mass $M_{total}$ is divided into the honeycomb shell $M_{shell}$ and the composite pivot shaft $M_{shaft}$:
	\begin{equation}
		M_{total} = (V_{total} \cdot \rho_{bulk}) + M_{shaft}
	\end{equation}
	An effective bulk density $\rho_{bulk} = 150 \text{ kg/m}^3$ was utilized to represent the combined weight of the hollow titanium honeycomb and composite skins. The pivot shaft mass was estimated at $60 \text{ kg}$ based on reported automated fiber placement (AFP) composite manufacturing data.
	
	The global Center of Gravity $(\bar{X}, \bar{Y})$ was determined using a weighted average of the geometric centroids of the individual panels and the localized mass of the pivot shaft:
	\begin{equation}
		\bar{X} = \frac{\sum (M_i \cdot x_i) + M_{shaft} \cdot x_{shaft}}{M_{total}}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		\bar{Y} = \frac{\sum (M_i \cdot y_i) + M_{shaft} \cdot y_{shaft}}{M_{total}}
	\end{equation}
	This approach ensures that the heavy internal structural members near the root are accounted for, shifting the CoG forward and inward relative to a purely aerodynamic centroid.
	
	The results were Total Mass:      277.5 kg (Shell: 217.5, Shaft: 60.0)
	CoG Location:    X = 1.958 m, Y = 0.791 m
	
	\subsection{EHA Simscape definition}
	
	\subsubsection{DC Motor}
	
	The electrical and mechanical parameters for the Parker GVM310-250-BA1-W motor were derived from the manufacturer's technical catalogs and fundamental physical relationships to establish an equivalent DC model for the simulation. The DC model is a simplification of reality in this case since the GVM310-250-BA1-W is a Permanent Magnet AC Motor.
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Armature Resistance ($R$):} The internal resistance was calculated using the rated mechanical power ($P_{rated} = 138 \text{ kW}$) and rated current ($I_{rated} = 370 \text{ A}_{rms}$) provided in the catalog. Using the standard power dissipation formula:
		$$R = \frac{P_{rated}}{I_{rated}^2} = \frac{138000}{370^2} \approx 0.001 \ \Omega$$
		
		\item \textbf{Armature Inductance ($L$):} Inductance was derived using the electrical time constant ($\tau_e = \frac{L}{R}$). Based on the manufacturer's engineering manual, the time constant for similar motors in this series scales slightly with stack length. Interpolating for a 250 mm stack length yields an estimated time constant of $\tau_e \approx 19.5 \text{ ms}$.
		$$L = \tau_e \cdot R = 0.0195 \cdot 0.001 = 1.95 \times 10^{-5} \text{ H}$$
		
		\item \textbf{Back EMF Constant ($K_e$):} The catalog specifies the Back EMF constant as $132 \text{ V}_{rms}/\text{krpm}$. To convert this to the required base unit of $\text{V/rpm}$ for the simulation block:
		$$K_e = \frac{132}{1000} = 0.132 \text{ V/rpm}$$
		
		\item \textbf{Rotor Inertia ($J$):} Since the exact inertia for the 310 mm frame was omitted from the summarized catalog, it was estimated by scaling the known inertia of the GVM210 series. Treating the rotor as a solid cylinder ($J \propto r^4 \cdot h$), the inertia scales linearly with stack length ($h$) and to the fourth power of the frame radius ($r$). Scaling from the GVM210-100 baseline ($J_{210\_100} = 0.0173 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$):
		$$J_{310\_250} = J_{210\_100} \cdot \left(\frac{250}{100}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{310}{210}\right)^4$$
		$$J_{310\_250} = 0.0173 \cdot 2.5 \cdot 4.747 \approx 0.205 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$$
		
		\item \textbf{Initialization Constants:} The rotor dampening ($Damp\_mtr$) and initial speed ($W\_init$) were set to 0 to represent ideal, stationary starting conditions. The viscous friction coefficient ($K\_vism$) was set to a nominal value of $1 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}/(\text{rad/s})$ to provide baseline numerical stability for the solver in the absence of empirical mechanical friction data. All three values are default values for the "DC Motor" Simscape block
	\end{itemize}
	
	\subsubsection{Pump}
	
	The fixed displacement pump parameters were derived from the performance requirements of the Electro-Hydrostatic Actuator (EHA) and the technical specifications of the selected Parker F12-162 bent-axis piston pump.
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Displacement ($D$):} The geometric displacement was selected directly from the manufacturer's datasheet to satisfy the high flow requirements of the system. For the F12-162 model, the displacement is fixed at:
		$$D = 163.1 \text{ cm}^3/\text{rev}$$
		
		\item \textbf{Nominal Pressure ($P_{nom}$):} The Parker F12 large-frame series is rated for a continuous maximum operating pressure of 420 bar (with intermittent peak limits up to 480 bar). This continuous rating is converted to Megapascals for the simulation block:
		$$P_{nom} = 42 \text{ MPa}$$
		
		\item \textbf{Nominal Shaft Angular Velocity ($W_{nom}$):} The required shaft speed is a function of the system's maximum flow rate requirement ($Q = 361.4 \text{ L/min}$) and the pump's displacement ($D$). Using the flow equation, the speed is determined as:
		$$W_{nom} = \frac{Q}{D} = \frac{361.4 \text{ L/min}}{163.1 \text{ cm}^3/\text{rev}} \cdot 1000 \approx 2216 \text{ RPM}$$
		This value falls safely within the pump's continuous operating envelope.
		
		\item \textbf{Efficiencies ($\eta_v$, $\eta_{hm}$):} To accurately model real-world flow and torque, the mathematical model incorporates volumetric ($\eta_v$) and mechanical ($\eta_{hm}$) efficiencies. Standard high-end baseline assumptions for bent-axis piston pumps at nominal conditions were applied:
		$$\eta_v = 0.92 \quad \text{and} \quad \eta_{hm} = 0.88$$
		
		\item \textbf{No-Load Torque ($T_{NL}$):} The no-load torque was initialized to $0 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$. This is a standard default value that allows the numerical solver to compute torque losses dynamically based entirely on the provided mechanical efficiency ($\eta_{hm}$) and the instantaneous fluid pressure, thereby avoiding conflicting constraints during simulation startup.
	\end{itemize}
	
	\subsubsection{Accumulator}
	
	The parameters for the gas-charged accumulator were derived from the geometric specifications of the selected Parker EHP 3-Liter piston accumulator and standard hydro-pneumatic sizing principles. To ensure numerical stability, the simulation was initialized at steady-state operating conditions to bypass initial compression transients.
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Total Volume ($V_{actot}$) \& Minimum Gas Volume ($V_{gasmin}$):} The total physical volume of the selected accumulator is 3 Liters ($3 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$). To prevent the piston from bottoming out against the fluid end cap during severe pressure spikes, a 5\% safety buffer was established for the minimum gas volume:
		$$V_{gasmin} = 0.05 \cdot 3 \times 10^{-3} = 1.5 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^3$$
		
		\item \textbf{Nominal Pressure ($P_{acnom}$):} The nominal operating pressure of the accumulator is equal to the baseline operating pressure of the EHA system.
		$$P_{acnom} = 20.7 \text{ MPa}$$
		
		\item \textbf{Precharge Pressure ($P_{pre}$):} According to standard accumulator sizing guidelines for energy storage applications, a piston accumulator should be precharged to 95\% of the system's minimum expected operating pressure \autocite{parker_accumulator_choice}. Assuming high-flow transient maneuvers cause the system pressure to drop momentarily to 150 bar (15 MPa), the precharge was calculated as:
		$$P_{pre} = 0.95 \cdot 15 \text{ MPa} = 14.25 \text{ MPa}$$
		
		\item \textbf{Nominal Gas Volume ($V_{gasnom}$):} The volume of the nitrogen gas when compressed to the nominal system pressure was calculated using Boyle's Law ($P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$), assuming isothermal compression from the unpressurized precharge state (where the gas occupies the total accumulator volume):
		$$P_{pre} \cdot V_{actot} = P_{acnom} \cdot V_{gasnom}$$
		$$V_{gasnom} = \frac{14.25 \cdot 3 \times 10^{-3}}{20.7} \approx 2.06 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$$
		
		\item \textbf{Initialization ($P_{aci}$, $V_{gasi}$):} To initialize the simulation at a pre-pressurized, steady-state equilibrium, the initial accumulator pressure and initial gas volume were set to equal their respective nominal conditions ($P_{aci} = 20.7 \text{ MPa}$ and $V_{gasi} = 2.06 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$).
	\end{itemize}
	
	\subsubsection{Valves}
	
	The EHA model utilizes check valves for anti-cavitation fluid replenishment and a pressure relief valve (PRV) for structural protection. The valve parameters were defined to accommodate the high flow rates of the system while prioritizing numerical stability in the simulation solver.
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Pressure Relief Valve Settings ($P_{set}$, $P_{reg}$):} The PRV prevents catastrophic over-pressurization during severe aerodynamic loading or piston hard-stop impacts. To ensure it does not prematurely bleed flow during standard operation, the cracking pressure ($P_{set}$) was established with a standard 15\% safety margin above the 20.7 MPa nominal system pressure:
		$$P_{set} = 20.7 \cdot 1.15 \approx 23.8 \text{ MPa}$$
		The pressure regulation range ($P_{reg}$), representing the differential required to transition the valve from just cracked to fully open, was assigned a standard hydraulic spring rate of approximately 10\% of the set pressure ($2.4 \text{ MPa}$).
		
		\item \textbf{Check Valve Pressures ($P_{crack}$, $P_{omax}$):} The check valves must immediately route fluid from the accumulator to the suction side of the pump to prevent cavitation. Consequently, the cracking pressure was set to a near-zero nominal value ($0.01 \text{ MPa}$) with a rapid full-opening threshold ($0.1 \text{ MPa}$) to ensure uninhibited flow recovery.
		
		\item \textbf{Maximum Opening Areas ($A_{max}$, $A_{omax}$):} Given the high maximum volumetric flow rate of the pump ($\approx 361 \text{ L/min}$), both the PRV and the check valves were assigned large maximum opening areas ($1 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2$). This ensures the valves can dump or replenish fluid at maximum flow without choking the system as restrictive orifices.
		
		\item \textbf{Numerical Stability Parameters ($F_{sm}$, $F_{psm}$):} Standard hydraulic orifice equations can introduce severe algebraic loops and zero-crossing errors in numerical solvers when valves rapidly switch states. To assist the simulation solver, the pressure-flow smoothing factors ($F_{sm}$, $F_{psm}$) were increased from their default to 0.05. Furthermore, higher-order dynamic phenomena, such as pressure recovery and opening dynamics, were disabled to isolate the macro-level kinematic response of the actuator.
	\end{itemize}
	
	\subsubsection{Actuator}

	The parameters for the asymmetrical double-acting hydraulic actuator were determined based on the physical geometry required to actuate the F-22 stabilator, combined with empirical friction data from the selected seal manufacturer.
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Chamber Areas ($A_A$, $A_B$):} The actuator requires a $0.2032 \text{ m}$ (8-inch) bore and a $0.0889 \text{ m}$ (3.5-inch) rod. The cross-sectional areas for the blank end (Chamber A) and rod end (Chamber B) are:
		$$A_A = \pi \left(\frac{0.2032}{2}\right)^2 = 0.0324 \text{ m}^2$$
		$$A_B = A_A - \pi \left(\frac{0.0889}{2}\right)^2 = 0.0262 \text{ m}^2$$
		
		\item \textbf{Friction Parameters ($F_{pre}$, $C_{fc}$):} Frictional forces were derived from the manufacturer's performance graphs for a KF-type seal with low gland friction. The empirical data, provided in imperial units (lbf and psi), was converted to SI units to calculate the Coulomb friction coefficient ($C_{fc}$) and the zero-pressure preload force ($F_{pre}$). 
		
		The total preload force is the sum of the seal and gland friction at 0 psi (see fig. \ref{fig:sealfrictiongraph} and \ref{fig:glandfrictiongraph}):
		$$F_{pre} = (30 \text{ lbf} + 35 \text{ lbf}) \cdot 4.448 \text{ N/lbf} = 289.1 \text{ N}$$
		
		The Coulomb friction force coefficient represents the linear slope of the friction-to-pressure relationship ($\frac{\Delta F}{\Delta P}$). Taking the average slope from 0 to 3000 psi (see fig. \ref{fig:sealfrictiongraph}):
		$$C_{fc} = \frac{(480 - 30) \text{ lbf} \cdot 4.448 \text{ N/lbf}}{3000 \text{ psi} \cdot 6894.76 \text{ Pa/psi}} \approx 9.68 \times 10^{-5} \text{ N/Pa}$$
		
		\begin{figure}[H]
			\centering
			\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{Media/Seal_friction_graph}
			\caption{Seal friction versus system pressure.}
			\label{fig:sealfrictiongraph}
		\end{figure}
		
		\begin{figure}[H]
			\centering
			\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{Media/Gland_Friction_graph}
			\caption{Gland friction versus system pressure.}
			\label{fig:glandfrictiongraph}
		\end{figure}
		
		\item \textbf{Leakage and Hard Stops:} To capture volumetric losses across the piston head, the piston-cylinder clearance was set to a nominal $0.1 \text{ mm}$ ($1 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}$) with a piston head length of $10 \text{ mm}$ ($0.01 \text{ m}$). The mechanical hard stops were initialized with a default high stiffness coefficient ($1 \times 10^{10} \text{ N/m}$) to represent rigid structural boundaries at the limits of the $0.1636 \text{ m}$ stroke.
		
		\item \textbf{Initial Conditions ($P_A$, $P_B$, $d_{ini}$):} To ensure the simulation initializes at a stable, steady-state equilibrium without transient startup shock, the pressures in Chambers A and B were balanced against the nominal system pressure ($P_{acnom} = 20.7 \text{ MPa}$). By setting $P_B = P_{acnom}$, the opposing pressure in Chamber A required to achieve a net-zero force ($F_{net} = P_A A_A - P_B A_B = 0$) is calculated as:
		$$P_A = P_{acnom} \cdot \frac{A_B}{A_A}$$
		The initial piston displacement ($d_{ini}$) was set to $0.0885 \text{ m}$, placing the actuator near its mid-stroke neutral position at $t=0$.
	\end{itemize}

	\subsubsection{Dynamic Mass}
	
	While static aerodynamic loads ($F_{ex}$) dictate the steady-state holding force required by the actuator, the dynamic control simulation must account for the inertial resistance of the stabilator during high-speed maneuvering. The force balance equation for the hydraulic jack (Equation \ref{eq:jack_eq}) includes the inertial term $M\ddot{x}_t$. To evaluate this properly, the rotational inertia of the stabilator must be reflected onto the linear axis of the actuator to define the equivalent linear mass ($M_{eq}$).
	
	Crucially, because the mechanical advantage or lever arm ($h_{act}$) changes as a function of the stabilator's deflection angle, the equivalent dynamic mass is not a static value. It is calculated dynamically across the deflection range using the varying lever arm and the total physical moving mass of the hydraulic cylinder rod ($m_{rod}$):
	
	\begin{equation}
		M_{eq}(\Delta \alpha) = \frac{I_{pivot}}{h_{act}(\Delta \alpha)^2} + m_{rod}
	\end{equation}
	
	Where $I_{pivot}$ is the mass moment of inertia of the stabilator around its pivot shaft, which can be approximated using a strip-theory integration of the stabilator's planform volume and bulk density. Industrial actuator catalogs typically specify total shipping weight rather than the dynamic moving mass. Thus, $m_{rod}$ is estimated volumetrically for a typical 8.00-inch bore and 3.50-inch rod cylinder machined from high-tensile steel ($\rho \approx 7850 \text{ kg/m}^3$). Assuming a standard piston thickness of 2.0 inches (0.0508 m) and a total physical rod length of 0.313 m (to accommodate the 163.61 mm stroke plus internal housing clearances and end joints), the moving masses are approximated as:
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Piston Mass:} $V_{piston} = \pi (0.1016 \text{ m})^2 (0.0508 \text{ m}) \approx 0.00165 \text{ m}^3 \rightarrow 12.9 \text{ kg}$
		\item \textbf{Rod Shaft Mass:} $V_{rod} = \pi (0.04445 \text{ m})^2 (0.313 \text{ m}) \approx 0.00194 \text{ m}^3 \rightarrow 15.2 \text{ kg}$
	\end{itemize}
	
	This yields a total estimated moving mass ($m_{rod}$) of approximately 28.1 kg. Because the lever arm $h_{act}$ is squared in the denominator of the reflection equation, dividing the stabilator's inertia by this small fraction massively amplifies the perceived mass at the actuator rod. To implement this non-linear inertial resistance in Simscape, $M_{eq}$ is represented dynamically as an array mapped to the actuator's displacement using a 1-D lookup table and a variable mass subsystem. This allows the simulation to accurately model the transient electrical currents and pressure spikes required to accelerate the system dynamically from any position.
	
	\subsubsection{Isothermal Liquid Properties}
	
	To accurately simulate the hydraulic fluid dynamics, the simulation's isothermal liquid properties were explicitly defined to match MIL-PRF-83282D \autocite{mil_prf_83282d}, the standard fire-resistant synthetic hydrocarbon hydraulic fluid used in modern US military aviation.
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Kinematic Viscosity ($IL_{Kvs}$):} The military specification mandates a minimum kinematic viscosity of 14.0 centistokes (cSt) at the nominal 40$^\circ$C operating temperature. Converting to standard SI units for the solver yields:
		$$IL_{Kvs} = 14.0 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2/\text{s}$$
		
		\item \textbf{Bulk Modulus ($IL_{bulk}$):} The isothermal secant bulk modulus dictates the fluid's compressibility, which directly impacts the actuator's dynamic stiffness. The specification requires a minimum bulk modulus of $1.379 \times 10^6 \text{ kPa}$ at 40$^\circ$C. Converting to Pascals:
		$$IL_{bulk} = 1.379 \times 10^9 \text{ Pa}$$
		
		\item \textbf{Density ($IL_{density}$):} The MIL-PRF-83282D standard dictates that the specific gravity of conformance batches must remain within $\pm 0.008$ of the manufacturer's originally qualified baseline. Therefore, a standard commercial baseline density for qualified fluids (such as AeroShell Fluid 31) was utilized:
		$$IL_{density} = 850 \text{ kg/m}^3$$
		
		\item \textbf{Solver Constants ($AP$, $P_{minv}$):} Atmospheric pressure ($AP$) was set to the standard $0.101325 \text{ MPa}$ to establish the baseline for gauge pressure calculations. To ensure the mathematical stability of the Simscape solver during extreme transient events (such as load-induced cavitation), the minimum valid absolute pressure ($P_{minv}$) was restricted to $1 \text{ Pa}$, preventing zero-crossing and infinite-state numerical divergence.
	\end{itemize}
